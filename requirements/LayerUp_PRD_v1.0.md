**Layer Up**

*Your daily weather briefing, delivered with personality.*

Product Requirements Document  |  v1.0  |  March 2026

# **1\. Product Overview**

Layer Up is a daily weather email newsletter that translates forecast data into plain-language, mildly humorous guidance for what to wear, what to bring, and how to move through the day. Where traditional weather apps give you a number, Layer Up tells you what to do with it.

| Vision | Be the first thing people read every morning — not because it's necessary, but because it's the only weather report that actually makes them smile. |
| :---: | :---- |

## **1.1  The Problem**

Standard weather apps and forecasts are accurate but emotionally flat. They give people data (58°F, 30% precipitation) but require the user to do all the interpretive work: Is that warm? Do I need a jacket? Should I wear my white sneakers? People — especially those doing multiple outdoor-to-indoor transitions throughout the day — consistently under- or over-dress because they're translating raw data without context.

## **1.2  The Solution**

A brief, personality-forward daily email that answers three questions before you ask them:

* What should I wear today?

* What should I bring with me?

* What quirks of today's weather should I plan around?

## **1.3  Target Audience**

| Segment | Description |
| :---- | :---- |
| Primary | Parents managing kids’ school-day outfits, remote workers who walk dogs or run midday errands, outdoor fitness enthusiasts |
| Secondary | Urban & suburban commuters, ages 25–55, who dress intentionally and spend time moving between indoor and outdoor environments |
| Geography | Initially US-only with ZIP code personalization; international expansion in v2 |
| Tone affinity | People who enjoy The Skimm, Morning Brew, or McSweeney's voice — smart, warm, and lightly self-aware |

## **1.4  Success Metrics**

| Metric | 6-Month Target |
| :---- | :---- |
| Email subscribers | 50,000 |
| Daily open rate | ≥ 45% (industry benchmark: \~22%) |
| Weekly retention (90-day) | ≥ 70% |
| Referral share rate | ≥ 8% of opens |
| Unsubscribe rate | ≤ 0.5% per send |
| NPS score | ≥ 65 |

# **2\. Feature Specification**

Features are organized by the daily email product (core) and the supporting website (acquisition and account management). Priority ratings are defined as: P0 \= launch blocker, P1 \= launch target, P2 \= post-launch v1.1, P3 \= future roadmap.

## **2.1  Daily Email: Core Sections**

### **2.1.1  Vibe of the Day**

| P0 | Vibe of the Day: One-line tone-setter that opens every email |
| :---: | :---- |

A single sentence at the top of every email that captures the emotional weather truth of the day — distinct from the forecast data. This sets the brand voice and gives readers an instant, shareable summary.

| Example | "Today is a cancel-everything-and-make-soup kind of Tuesday." | "Genuinely gorgeous out there. No notes." |
| :---: | :---- |

* Generated by combining temperature, precipitation, wind, and season into a tone-matched template library

* \~15 base vibe archetypes, each with 4–6 variants to avoid repetition

* Seasonally calibrated ("soup weather" only triggers Oct–Mar, etc.)

### **2.1.2  Temperature Translation**

| P0 | Temperature Translation: Plain-language temp interpretation with feels-like callout |
| :---: | :---- |

Shows actual high/low and feels-like temperature with an opinionated, human take on what that number means in practice.

| Example | "It'll hit 34°F, but with wind chill it'll feel like 22°F. The gap between those two numbers is the gap between your dignity and a miserable commute." |
| :---: | :---- |

* Displays both actual and feels-like prominently

* Flags significant feels-like divergence (\>8°F difference) with a dedicated callout

* Uses colloquial temperature labels ("hoodie weather," "face-hurting cold," "suspiciously pleasant")

### **2.1.3  Layering Guidance**

| P0 | Layering Guidance: Context-aware outfit recommendations for two activity modes |
| :---: | :---- |

The product's signature feature. Two distinct layering recommendations based on how the reader will spend their day, acknowledging that spending an hour outside running is fundamentally different from running in and out of a heated car.

| Mode | Logic |
| :---- | :---- |
| Outdoor / Walking Mode | Extended outdoor exposure — dog walks, commuting on foot, outdoor workouts. More layers, wind/moisture protection prioritized, hat/gloves included at lower temps. |
| Errand Mode | Car-to-building-to-car patterns. Fewer layers (you'll overheat inside). Emphasis on easy-on/off jacket over bulky coats. Scarf over hat. |

* Each mode produces a 2–4 item outfit suggestion with brief humorous rationale

* Jacket/coat type specified (light layer, fleece, insulated, heavy winter coat)

* Layering logic accounts for wind chill, humidity, and precipitation probability

* Iconography differentiates the two modes visually in email rendering

### **2.1.4  Footwear Recommendation**

| P0 | Footwear Recommendation: Shoe guidance based on ground conditions and precipitation |
| :---: | :---- |

A dedicated shoe recommendation that accounts for precipitation, snowmelt, puddle risk, and temperature — because white sneakers deserve protection.

| Example | "Leave the white sneakers at home. Snowmelt \+ road salt \= a beige nightmare. Waterproof or bust today." |
| :---: | :---- |

| Condition | Recommendation |
| :---- | :---- |
| Rain (\>40% prob.) | Waterproof shoes or rain boots |
| Snow / ice | Waterproof \+ grip. No exceptions. |
| Snowmelt (above 32°F post-snow) | Waterproof recommended; salt staining risk callout |
| Dry \+ cold | Any closed-toe will do; ankle coverage suggested below 20°F |
| Warm \+ dry | Go nuts, wear the sandals |

* Footwear logic is independent from the layering guidance section

* Humor is calibrated — light ribbing about fashion choices, never condescending

### **2.1.5  Accessory Checklist**

| P0 | Accessory Checklist: Sunglasses, umbrella, hat/gloves, and other daily bag items |
| :---: | :---- |

A quick-scan checklist of items to grab before leaving the house, each triggered by weather conditions.

| Item | Trigger Condition |
| :---- | :---- |
| Sunglasses | UV index ≥ 3 OR post-storm glare risk (bright after clouds/snow) |
| Umbrella | Precipitation probability ≥ 30% |
| Hat (warm) | Feels-like ≤ 28°F or wind \> 20 mph below 40°F |
| Gloves | Feels-like ≤ 32°F |
| Scarf | Wind \> 15 mph below 38°F |
| Sunscreen reminder | UV index ≥ 6 (summer months, or high altitude) |
| Hand lotion note | Humidity \< 25% in winter |

* Each item includes a one-line comment when triggered ("Your umbrella WILL blow inside out at 4pm. Bring the sturdy one.")

* Items are suppressed when not applicable — no empty sections

### **2.1.6  Precipitation Timing**

| P1 | Precipitation Timing: Hourly rain/snow windows with practical scheduling nudges |
| :---: | :---- |

When precipitation is forecast, the email translates hourly data into human scheduling advice rather than just a probability percentage.

| Example | "Rain rolls in around 2pm and parks itself there until 7pm. Morning errands are your friend. Afternoon is a write-off." |
| :---: | :---- |

* Identifies precipitation windows in 2-hour blocks

* Generates a "best time to be outside" recommendation

* Distinguishes between brief showers ("bring an umbrella just in case") and sustained rain ("reschedule anything outdoors")

### **2.1.7  Activity Suitability Ratings**

| P1 | Activity Ratings: Quick icons scoring 4–6 common activities for today's conditions |
| :---: | :---- |

A scannable section with green/yellow/red ratings (or 1–5 stars) for a fixed set of activities, so readers can make quick go/no-go decisions.

| Activity | Factors Considered |
| :---- | :---- |
| Dog walk | Feels-like temp, precipitation, wind, ice risk |
| Outdoor run / exercise | Temp, humidity, wind, UV, air quality |
| Outdoor dining / patio | Temp, wind, precipitation, evening temp drop |
| Yard work / gardening | Ground conditions, wind, temp, rain timing |
| Kid outdoor play | More conservative thresholds; ice/wind weighted heavily |
| Bike commute | Wind direction, precipitation, road conditions |

* Users can customize which 4 activities appear in their email (v1.1 personalization)

* Each rating includes a 1-line reason ("Too icy for outdoor dining, but the patio heaters make it a maybe")

### **2.1.8  UV Index Plain-Language Translation**

| P1 | UV Translation: Human-readable UV guidance replacing index numbers |
| :---: | :---- |

| Example | "UV index today: 8\. That's 'you will absolutely burn in 20 minutes' territory. Sunscreen is not optional." |
| :---: | :---- |

* 5-tier UV language system: Non-issue / Be aware / Wear SPF / Sunscreen mandatory / Actual hazard

* Triggered year-round, with winter callouts for high-altitude and post-snow glare

* Suppressed on overcast days unless index still elevated (possible in cloud cover)

### **2.1.9  The Dew Point Dispatch (Dad’s Corner)**

A daily dew point callout written for people who have definitely heard about the dew point from a family member and still do not fully understand it. The goal is not to explain it exhaustively — the goal is to make it feel useful, relatable, and just a little bit funny.

*The short version: dew point is the temperature at which moisture condenses out of the air. High dew point \= muggy, sweaty, hair-ruining misery. Low dew point \= dry, crispy, static-electricity-and-chapped-lips territory. It’s actually a better indicator of how uncomfortable you’ll feel outside than humidity percentage — your father is, annoyingly, correct about this.*

| Dew Point Range | Vibe Label | Example Copy |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Below 35°F | Dry & Crispy | “Lotion situation today. Static and chapped lips incoming.” |
| 35°F – 49°F | Comfortable | “Your dad would call this ‘perfect’ dew point. He’s not wrong.” |
| 50°F – 59°F | Noticeably Muggy | “The air has opinions today. Your hair will agree.” |
| 60°F – 65°F | Uncomfortable | “You will sweat doing absolutely nothing. Dress accordingly.” |
| Above 65°F | Oppressive | “Your dad is outside right now calling this ‘real humidity.’ He is correct and you should stay inside.” |

* Section appears daily; the copy template rotates through 5 variants per tier to avoid repetition

* Section header reads “Dad’s Corner” in the email (collapsible/opt-out for non-dad-havers in v1.2)

* Dew point data pulled from same weather API feed as temperature; no additional API cost

* High dew point days (≥60°F) trigger a frizz warning in the Accessory section for hair-care awareness

### **2.1.10  Weekend Weather Preview (Friday Edition)**

| P2 | Friday Preview: Saturday and Sunday summary included in Friday emails |
| :---: | :---- |

Friday emails include a two-day preview of the weekend — same voice, condensed format — so readers can plan outdoor activities, events, or attire ahead of time.

* Pulls 48-hour forecast for Saturday and Sunday

* Condensed to single-paragraph per day: vibe, temp range, precipitation

* Includes one weekend-specific recommendation ("Sunday is your only window for outdoor time this weekend")

# **3\. Technical Architecture**

## **3.1  Weather Data**

| Component | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| Primary API | Open-Meteo (free, no key required) or Tomorrow.io (paid, higher accuracy) |
| Fallback API | National Weather Service API (US-only, free, official) |
| Data refresh | Forecast pulled nightly at 4:00 AM local time per ZIP code cluster |
| Inputs used | Temperature (actual \+ feels-like), precipitation probability \+ type, hourly precip timing, wind speed \+ direction, UV index, humidity, cloud cover, sunrise/sunset |
| ZIP resolution | ZIP-to-lat/lon lookup table; cluster nearby ZIPs to reduce API calls |

## **3.2  Email Generation & Delivery**

| Component | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| Template engine | MJML (responsive email HTML) compiled to ESP-ready HTML |
| Copy generation | Rule-based template system with conditional logic for all weather variables; AI assist for vibe copy (optional enhancement) |
| ESP | SendGrid or Mailchimp (TBD); must support scheduled sends, segmentation by timezone, and unsubscribe management |
| Send time | Staggered by user timezone; default 7:00 AM local; user-adjustable |
| Deliverability | DKIM, SPF, DMARC configured; warm-up schedule for new sending domain |

## **3.3  Website Stack**

* Frontend: Next.js (React) with Tailwind CSS

* Backend: Node.js API routes for sign-up, preferences, and referral tracking

* Database: PostgreSQL (subscriber records, preferences, referral tracking)

* Hosting: Vercel (frontend) \+ Railway or Render (API/database)

* Analytics: Plausible (privacy-first, GDPR compliant)

## **3.4  Data Privacy**

* Subscriber data stored: email, ZIP code, name (optional), preferences, referral code

* No third-party data sharing or advertising network integration

* GDPR-compliant unsubscribe and data deletion requests

* No tracking pixels beyond open/click tracking standard to ESP

# **4\. Content & Voice Guidelines**

## **4.1  Brand Voice**

Layer Up reads like a smart, slightly sleep-deprived friend who checked the weather so you didn't have to. The tone is warm, a little wry, and always on your side. It pokes fun at the weather — not at the reader.

| We are | We are not |
| :---- | :---- |
| Mildly humorous — a smirk, not a punchline | Trying to be a stand-up comedian |
| Opinionated ("wear the boots") | Wishy-washy ("you might want to consider") |
| Concise — max 300 words per email | Verbose or lecture-y |
| On the reader's side | Condescending about fashion or choices |
| Seasonally self-aware (no summer jokes in January) | Tone-deaf to regional context |

## **4.2  Humor Guardrails**

* Never mock the reader's choices, only the weather's behavior

* Humidity and wind chill are reliable comedic targets

* Seasonal incongruity (70°F in March) is always funny

* Avoid humor about severe weather events — safety-mode copy when storms, extreme cold, or heat advisories are active

* Dad Joke Mode must be opt-in, never default

## **4.3  Safety Mode**

When the National Weather Service issues a watch, warning, or advisory for the user's area, the email switches to Safety Mode: humor is removed, the advisory is displayed prominently, and practical safety guidance replaces standard outfit content.

| Triggers | NWS alerts for: winter storm, ice storm, tornado watch/warning, heat advisory/excessive heat, air quality alerts, flood watch/warning |
